> Can you tell us a little about yourself -- your background, current role, why you did the MBA, and what drives you?

I grew up in Perth, Western Australia. Studied electrical engineering and computer science, completed a Master of Professional Engineering at UWA, and went straight into the construction and infrastructure consulting industry at Stantec -- a global firm of about 32,000 people. I stayed for almost ten years and moved up fairly quickly, mostly by being willing to become whatever the organisation needed next. When they needed an ICT specialist, I became that. Then smart cities. Then audiovisual engineering. Then renewable energy. I ended up as a Principal leading the national solar and battery team, managing a multi-million dollar P&L.

What drives me -- honestly -- is that I like to do things so well it stops being "well" and starts being unreasonable. I like to crush it, to blow people away. If I'm being psychoanalytically honest about that, what it probably means is that a primary motivator is external approval, and I try not to examine that too closely.

The MBA was partly an expression of that impulse. Going to the top-ranked MBA in the country, becoming class Co-President, exchanging at an Ivy League school, sitting twenty-two courses when the normal load is twelve, graduating Dux -- those were things that I could see a future version of me being well-regarded and highly-appraised by others for doing. But it was also about something less unflattering and possibly more relevant to talk about: after ten years in one career you start to feel defined by it, and I'd always felt like only about twenty percent of my self-expression could happen through engineering. I love too many other things -- poetry, finance, history, community leadership, programming, writing -- and an MBA is a degree that is deliberately as broad as a degree can be. It's the toolkit to go out into the world and do whatever you want, well. For a person who was 50% "my hobby is having hobbies" and 50% <q>I need to crack the mind-forg'd manacles,</q> it was a great fit.

Flaubert would recognise the shape of it, actually. Emma Bovary's tragedy is that her cage is imposed entirely from the outside -- society cultivates her aspirations and then refuses her any agency to act on them. My version was less severe, more self-inflicted: I sensed a box being drawn slowly around me, competence in one domain being traded into a terminal identity, and felt suffocated by it. The MBA was partly about breaking out of that box, and partly about building a wider one.

> How do you define gender equity in your own words?

I've gone back and forth on this. The clean answer is something about removing structural barriers so that talent determines outcomes -- and I do believe that, but it's the kind of sentence that sounds right without asking very much at all of the person saying it.

The more honest answer is that equity is what's left when you strip away the things a society has confused for nature. Wollstonecraft made this argument in [<cite>A Vindication of the Rights of Woman</cite>](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/A_Vindication_of_the_Rights_of_Woman) in 1792 and it still hasn't fully landed: what her contemporaries saw as innate feminine weakness, she saw as the predictable consequence of denied education and enforced dependence. The patterns have updated -- only the extreme fringes of the world argue that women shouldn't be educated anymore -- but the underlying mechanism is the same. We observe a disparity, attribute it to something essential about the people involved, and then build systems that reproduce the disparity, which we then take as further evidence of its naturalness. It's self-fulfilling prophecy disguised as shrewd, realist observation.

So if I had to distil it: gender equity is the discipline of asking, before you accept any difference in outcome as natural, whether you've properly ruled out structural explanations. Most of the time, we haven't.

> From your perspective, what are some of the most significant challenges women continue to face in business today?

The challenges that persist are the ones that present as neutral features of the environment. Overt exclusion is increasingly rare and easy to name when it occurs -- and naming something is half the work of addressing it. The harder problems are the ones that don't register as problems at all.

Competence in men tends to be the default assumption until disproven. For women, it's more often something to be established, repeatedly, in each new room. This isn't anyone's policy. It's rarely even a conscious attitude. It's ambient. And because it's ambient, it's exhausting in a way that doesn't show up in any metric or exit interview.

Plath's fig tree in [<cite>The Bell Jar</cite>](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/The_Bell_Jar) gets cited often in this context, usually as a metaphor for work-life balance. But I think the more interesting reading is about perception rather than logistics. Esther isn't paralysed because she literally can't pursue multiple things -- she's paralysed because the options have been framed as mutually exclusive for her in a way they wouldn't be for a man in the same position. The constraint isn't practical. It's conceptual. And conceptual constraints are harder to dismantle than practical ones, because they don't look like constraints -- they look like reality.

There's a related phenomenon I'd call meritocracy paradox. Organisations that believe most fervently that they are meritocracies tend to be the least likely to examine whether their systems actually produce meritocratic outcomes. The belief itself wards against scrutiny. That's worth sitting with, because most of us work in places that would describe themselves this way.

> Who do you admire for how they're modelling inclusive leadership?

Firstly, my high school literature teacher who had no hesitation answering a political question by telling his inquirer that, yes, his viewpoint was probably best described as Marxist-feminist, and no he wasn't particularly concerned if anyone had a problem with that because he'd learned back in England to check boys' knuckles when they walked in, and he'd already determined that nobody here was a threat to him. An early example of masculinity displayed in the form of strident rejection of unjustness everywhere even when only subtly insinuated; and a valuable lesson for a young man in the early 2010s to be sure.

Beyond that, I would want to redirect this slightly, because I think the question assumes inclusive leadership is a specific set of practices, and I'm not sure that's right.

To me, inclusive leadership means leading in a way that enables anyone -- regardless of their innate characteristics -- to be successful. When you frame it that way, the scope gets much wider than acts of commission. "Anyone regardless of their innate characteristics" includes whole swaths of people who find aspects of the modern corporate environment genuinely difficult or even traumatising, for myriad reasons, many of them more than entirely understandable. And you can't hope to taxonomise every situation you might need to be accommodating about, in advance. It's not a box-ticking exercise -- braille on the doors, sleep pods for people who had a rough night, breastfeeding rooms, multi-faith room, right, all sorted -- now back to leveraging the thinly veiled implication of financial dependence at risk to drive results. It's obvious when we name it, but it's discomforting how common it is.

Because you can't predict all the ways you might need to be inclusive, the only true strategy I think is being a person that is genuinely warm, affable, and trustworthy. That sounds simple, but it's surprisingly rare in leadership, because a lot of people in senior positions have internalised the idea that authority requires a degree of austerity -- that respect and approachability are in tension. I'd encourage anyone who holds that belief to question earnestly the true level of association between respect and harshness. I don't think they need to be connected. The leaders I've admired most were the ones who were easy to talk to like friends, and whose teams performed well precisely because people weren't spending energy managing around their manager's temperament (unless out of positive affect rather than negative).

> How have you seen gender equity reflected (or not) in your workplace or in your studies?

The honest answer is that for most of my engineering career, I didn't see it clearly at all -- and I think the not-seeing is more the point worth making.

When you're inside a system its imbalances just look like the shape of things. I spent nearly ten years in engineering consulting where the gender ratio starts dire at entry and narrows at every level above. I registered it the way you register the paint on the wall -- it was there, it was obvious if you looked directly at it, but it wasn't something I was actively processing.

The shift started the first time I interviewed a candidate. I didn't like her. And my immediate next thought was: is that gender bias? That would be horrible. But genuinely, she wasn't a good fit. I remember calling my manager to ask for his advice -- not because I couldn't make the decision, but because the weight of it had suddenly become real in a way it hadn't been before. Being in a position of authority over someone's prospects does that. It makes fairness a live concern rather than an abstract value, and it opens your eyes much more widely than working in the rank and file does.

What followed from that doubt was more useful than any training module. Once you start questioning your own judgement in one context, you start noticing how many conclusions go unexamined in a uniform group -- how much intellectual debt a homogeneous team carries without knowing it, how many assumptions it treats as settled that are actually just uncontested. All of that arises out of doubt. Doubt, I think, is enormously useful -- and undervalued in professional cultures that reward decisiveness above almost everything else.

> Can you share an example where you saw women being empowered in your field, and how you or others contributed as allies?

Over my time at Stantec I mentored ten people, two of whom were women, about five years apart. They were a study in contrasts. One was an average candidate, who moved quickly toward opportunity -- assertive, visible, good at positioning herself. The other was exceptionally talented and performing well above her level -- but didn't go out of her way to highlight it. Regardless, each went on to reach a similar level of success on a similar timeline.

What struck me was that each person's aptitude for self-advocacy -- the thing we spend enormous energy talking about societally -- turned out to be fairly low in the order of things that were materially consequential to their outcomes. That observation unsettled a few of my assumptions.

It crystallised, in my understanding, as the difference between biases and what I'd call tropes. We've become reasonably literate about biases -- prejudicial or stereotypical ideas that distort our judgement of people. What we're less equipped to notice are tropes: narrative templates that shape how we perceive a situation before conscious judgement even begins. They don't characterise a class of people the way biases do. They're subtler. The mere idea that some pattern exists can lure you into matching your observations to it. It's lazy thinking, essentially, but it can cause you to do yourself and others a real disservice.

"Imposter syndrome" was one such frame, and I watched it steal real energy and attention from the second woman I mentored. She'd spoken about it occasionally as something she was concerned about -- and that concern, that self-monitoring, the internal witness critiquing the internal witness, was itself the cost. The off-the-shelf memetic package didn't actually describe her reality, but it became part of her reality by occupying space that should have gone towards other things. I'd encourage anyone who's heard the term to watch Reshma Saujani's [speech on "Bicycle Face"](https://www.ted.com/talks/reshma_saujani_teach_girls_bravery_not_perfection) as an inoculant, but notice that even when we disabuse ourselves of a trope like this, it has still consumed time and focus we don't get back.

As her manager, I think the most useful thing I did was signpost it once, then desist from engaging with it further -- to keep the conversation on her work, which was excellent, and to treat her trajectory as a matter of engineering and relationship-building as a consultant, rather than personal psychology. She came into her own, learned to advocate for herself, and found success.

I'm cautious about making that a tidy story about mentorship that advises men not to answer metacognitive questions from women on their staff; the structural observation is more important than the specific personal one in this example. Self-advocacy mattered less than I'd been led to believe, and the narrative presuppositions we apply to women's experiences are often more distorting than clarifying, even the ones we thought were helpful.

> If you could go back and talk to a younger version of yourself about gender equity, what advice would you share?

I'd tell him to pay closer attention to what his reading was actually telling him. I mentioned [<cite>Madame Bovary</cite>](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Madame_Bovary) earlier -- it took me years to notice that Flaubert wrote a woman's constrained experience with more precision and empathy than most of the men in Emma's life could manage, and that this wasn't incidental to his talent but central to it. The capacity to inhabit someone else's structural reality, rather than just acknowledge it exists, is a specific skill. It requires sustained attention, not just sympathy.

I'd also tell him to re-read [<cite>The Awakening</cite>](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/The_Awakening_(Chopin_novel)) after class, and this time sit with the discomfort of it instead of trying to assign literary meaning like the curriculum wanted him to. What's unsettling about Chopin's novel isn't the ending or the metaphor of the amniotic embrace of the ocean which seems to be a common motif in the feminist canon -- it's the absence of a villain. The system that constrains Edna Pontellier isn't malicious. Nobody is scheming against her. The people around her are, by their own lights, being perfectly reasonable. And yet the net effect is suffocating. That's closer to how structural inequity actually operates than any story with a clear antagonist or a moral. The uncomfortable realisation for men reading it is that you don't have to be the villain to be part of the architecture of wrong.

> What is one practical action men can take this year to support gender equity?

I'd start by refuting, gently, the way this question typically gets answered. The standard advice tends toward monitoring who speaks in meetings, tracking interruptions and speaking up for people who weren't given their fair share of airtime. I understand the intent, and the argumentation around microaggression is cogent, but I've moved towards a view of many of these things as being a tyranny of low expectations.

I'm uneasy saying that, but I think it's true: it keeps the conversation at the level of etiquette when the more consequential work is cognitive and structural. On the structural front, to illustrate, while Woolf's [<cite>A Room of One's Own</cite>](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/A_Room_of_One%27s_Own) usually gets reduced to the sentimental "500 pounds" reading about money and space, her actual argument is far more interesting: she's not just saying women need resources -- she's tracing the relationship between material independence and intellectual freedom, and showing that the second is impossible without the first in ways that are invisible to anyone who's always had both. If you've never had to negotiate for the basic conditions of serious work, you're unlikely to notice that others are negotiating for them constantly. The workplace equivalent is worth thinking about: who in your organisation has all the structural conditions to do their best work -- say, access to the team members they need, and not only at times when nobody else needs them -- and meanwhile who is spending energy navigating conditions like this that others don't even see? That's a more useful question than the ones we usually ask.

On the conceptual, a hard but valuable thing you could do is examine your perceptual scaffolding -- the tropes and frames you bring to a situation before conscious judgement begins. When you see a woman who's quiet in meetings, do you reach for "confidence issue"? When you see one who's assertive, do you pattern-match to a different set of assumptions? And are you blind to the same for men -- like Wallace's [fish oblivious to water](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/This_Is_Water). Those abstractions are harder to audit than surface behaviours, but they're where the real distortions live.

> As we reflect on International Women's Day, is there a message or story you'd like to share about allyship and supporting women?

The thing I keep returning to, having thought through all of these questions, is how much of the real damage is done by things we adopt in good faith. Biases get the attention because they're identifiable and actionable -- you can name them, train against them, build processes to counteract them. Not to mention that they're emotionally temptacious, coming prepackaged and prefixed with the "unconscious" excuse that readily assuages guilt through prophylactic absolution.

But they distract us from tropes, which are harder to see and, I suspect, more consequential. As a result the well-intentioned focus on bias becomes perfunctory and performative, prevents us from addressing deeper problems, and cost everyone -- the people to whom they're applied most of all, but also the organisations and teams that make worse decisions without ever quite noticing the connection.

<div class="pull-quote">
She did not wish women to have power over men, but over themselves.
<span class="attribution">Mary Wollstonecraft</span>
</div>

What strikes me about that sentence is how precisely it identifies where the work actually lies. It's not about transferring power or the solidarity of a moral underwriter. It's about clearing away the accumulated structures, assumptions, and lazy frames that prevent people from exercising agency they should not rightly have to struggle for.

I don't have a tidy conclusion for that. I think the honest position is that this is a genuinely difficult problem that needs sustained effort, and anyone who tells you otherwise is probably selling something simpler than the truth. But the people I've watched do this work well -- really well -- share two things in common: they vigilantly warded off certainty, never allowing themselves to circumscribe or concise the issue and thereby foreclose on new elements of it that might later come to light. And they were eminently kind. If I've learned anything worth passing on, it's probably that.
